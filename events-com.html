01 — The Platform

Complex by nature.
Failing silently.

Events.com is an all-in-one SaaS platform for event organizers — ticketing, marketing, analytics, and attendee management from a single interface. The user base spans enterprise clients with dedicated success managers, independent organizers running everything themselves, and first-time users who have never touched the product. Every design decision has to work across that range.

I was embedded across the full design lifecycle — user research, competitive analysis, information architecture, and high-fidelity prototyping in Axure RP. Alongside a separate analytics framework project, the primary focus was a problem the platform couldn't see clearly: self-service users were failing to activate on their own, and the customer success team was absorbing the consequences.

The goal was not to patch individual screens. It was to find out exactly where the platform was breaking down, and build the case for fixing it systematically.

02 — The Research

No research team.
Built the methodology from scratch.

There was no dedicated research function. Every stage of the study had to be designed and run from scratch — not just facilitating sessions, but building the infrastructure that would make the findings usable by product and engineering without a translator between them.

Participant Recruitment
Identified and recruited participants across self-service user segments. Handled all communication, scheduling, and logistics independently — no coordinator, no ops support.
Script Writing
Wrote both remote and in-person usability scripts from scratch. Each script was structured around task completion and failure detection — not satisfaction ratings. The goal was to watch what users actually did, not what they said they would do.
Session Facilitation
Conducted remote sessions via appear.in and in-person sessions directly. Documented participant pathways step by step — each action timestamped, each observation noted, each verbatim quote captured alongside the screen state it came from.
Heuristic Audit
Ran a systematic heuristic evaluation across the full platform in parallel. Every violation catalogued by category and severity — giving the usability session findings a structural framework rather than a disconnected list.

03 — The Findings

55 findings. Every one
tagged, rated, and traceable.

The usability sessions and heuristic audit produced 55 findings total. Each one was categorized against NNg heuristics, assigned a severity level, and tied to a specific page, scenario, and participant. The spreadsheet was the deliverable — not a deck summarizing themes, but a complete record engineering could reference directly.

Every finding documented: page, participant, heuristic category, severity rating, and screenshot reference. 55 rows, each one actionable.
Severity distribution across all 55 findings
21
Level 1 — Cosmetic
Fix when time permits. Real friction points, but users could still complete tasks — just with more effort and uncertainty than necessary.
22
Level 2 — Serious
High priority. Users delayed significantly or chose incorrect paths. These were the issues that generated CS escalations — fixable without a system rebuild.
11
Level 3 — Critical
Must fix. Task failure or complete abandonment. The findings that pointed directly at structural gaps — not interface polish, but missing systems.

37.8% of findings fell under a single heuristic category: Help and Documentation. 20% under Error Prevention. Together they accounted for more than half of all violations — and they weren't random. They were concentrated in the exact moments where a new self-service user would be deciding whether to continue or give up. Post-study surveys triangulated the same point: participants suggested video tutorials, hovering text, and interactive walkthroughs unprompted.

04 — Research to Roadmap

Findings that engineering
could act on without a meeting.

The most common failure in research-heavy design work isn't the research — it's the handoff. Findings get presented, nodded at, and deprioritized because they arrive in a format that doesn't translate directly to engineering work. Every finding from this study was converted into a JIRA story shell, scored by severity and undertaking, and structured so the team could sequence and ship without needing design present at each decision.

JIRA artifacts, Axure prototypes, and internal product screens are not shown here. Events.com operated in a competitive SaaS market and was sensitive about tooling and workflow details being visible externally. The research documentation shown in sections 02 and 03 was cleared for portfolio use; internal delivery artifacts were not.

Low Undertaking — Immediate Action

Findings converted directly to JIRA story shells

Every Level 1 and Level 2 finding with a contained fix was written up as a JIRA story shell: the specific screen, the participant scenario it came from, the severity rating, and the design recommendation. Engineering could verify, scope, and ship each one without a design review gate for routine items.

→ Outcome

All small-undertaking usability enhancements were developed and shipped. The structured documentation meant zero context was lost between the research session and the production fix.

Every low-undertaking finding reached production — zero lost in handoff
High Undertaking — Separate Initiative

The 37.8% required a system, not a patch

The Help and Documentation concentration wasn't a collection of individual bugs — it was a structural signal. The platform had no onboarding layer. Writing twenty individual JIRA stories would have produced twenty small fixes for a problem that was architectural. The right response was a different kind of project entirely.

→ Decision

Separated into a standalone initiative. Co-led the end-to-end UX strategy and implementation of an automated onboarding tool built around the exact failure modes the research had surfaced.

Documented increase in help & documentation accessibility after launch
What was specified and built
01
Messages & Modals
First-time user welcome flow and contextual modals at high-failure moments. Designed to intercept users before they reached the dead ends the research had documented.
02
Onboarding Checklists
Progressive task lists guiding new organizers through the critical setup path. Gave users a visible sense of progress and made the next step explicit at every stage.
03
Walkthroughs & Call-outs
Contextual, triggerable walkthroughs for existing users who needed help mid-task. Addressed the post-study survey finding: participants asked for hovering text and interactive tutorials unprompted.
The Outcome

55 findings. A self-sustaining
pipeline. Everything shipped.

The research infrastructure was built to outlast the project. When the onboarding tool launched, the documentation gap that had driven 37.8% of all findings had a system behind it — not a collection of individual patches.

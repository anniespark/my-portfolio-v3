01 — The Role

Missionary. Designer.
Both at once.

Acts2 Network is a Christian nonprofit with missionary teams across Asia. Journey Taipei is its Taipei chapter — community, events, and Bible study for university students. I joined as a paid missionary. My specific job was design and growth.

The Reality

A 10-person cross-functional team, all with full-time roles, running events, mentoring students, and producing content simultaneously. No design department. No content team. No inherited playbook from whoever had this role before.

It felt like a startup — everyone wore many hats, nothing was defined in advance, and the work didn't stop at 5pm. What it required was figuring out what the community needed, building the system to deliver it, and doing it fast enough to matter.

The Two Jobs

Design & Growth Lead — owned the Journey Taipei Instagram: strategy, visual identity, content creation (posts, posters, reels, t-shirts), and analytics. Responsible for growing the account and driving sign-ups to events.

Campus Co-Leader, Fu Jen University — co-led the FJU campus community with my husband. Event planning, student mentorship, talks, and whatever else needed doing.

02 — Design & Growth

Growing an Instagram with
AI as a coworker.

The account needed consistent, bilingual content that resonated with Taiwanese university students — a specific audience with specific cultural context. I didn't have a team of writers or a content strategist. I had Perplexity.

"Perplexity wasn't a search tool. It was the coworker I didn't have — one I could pitch ideas to, go back and forth with, and get to a final proposal before I ever opened Figma."

California → Taipei

New environment.
Fast adaptation.

I moved from California to Taipei without speaking Mandarin. I navigated a new culture, a new team, and a new community — while raising a child. The speed of that adaptation wasn't accidental. It came from treating every interaction as something worth paying attention to.

What I learned: how to read a room you didn't grow up in, navigate conflict across cultural norms, and make calls when there's no playbook.

The design workshop — two hours, built for whoever takes this role after me — is probably the thing I'm most proud of. Not the metrics. The fact that I thought about what happens when I leave.

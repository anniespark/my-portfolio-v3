01 — Context

The community is the product.

Acts2 Network is a Christian nonprofit operating a network of 125+ ministry teams and 1,600+ co-vocational ministers — people with day jobs who serve full-time in building community. Journey Taipei is its campus vertical at Fu Jen University: a student-focused community welcoming university and master's students — including non-Christians — into events, Bible study, and faith exploration.

The Org

Acts2 operates on a "Way of Life" model — 15 spiritual principles enacted through campus ministries, church plants, discipleship programs, and mission trips across Asia. It's a decentralized network: each vertical owns its own local execution within a shared framework.

Journey Taipei's audience was twofold: existing community members who needed a reason to stay engaged, and the broader student population of Taipei who didn't yet know the community existed.

My Role

As the most experienced designer on a 10-person cross-functional team, I was brought in not to post content — but to architect the system that determined how the community grew, how it communicated, and how it turned digital reach into real-world participation.

I treated the community as a product: the Instagram account was the acquisition funnel, the events were the activation layer, and the 1-on-1 student sessions were user research. Every growth decision was made the same way I'd make a product decision — with data, a hypothesis, and a measurable outcome.

02 — The System Problem

A leaky funnel with
no system behind it.

"The digital presence wasn't converting passive reach into active community. Traffic existed. Belonging didn't — yet."

01
No Content Strategy

Posts without a system, reach without intent

The organization had a digital presence but no data-driven content strategy behind it. Posts were reactive, not designed to move a specific audience through a defined journey — from discovery to event attendance to community membership.

02
Leaky Engagement Model

Passive reach not converting to participation

People were seeing the content but not showing up. The gap between digital visibility and physical attendance was wide — and unmeasured. There was no mechanism to understand why people who engaged online didn't take the next step in person.

03
No Execution Velocity

A lean team with no force multiplier

A 10-person volunteer team with day jobs can't produce agency-level content output without a system. There was no shared design language, no content cadence, and no tooling strategy. The execution ceiling was low — and getting lower as the team's bandwidth compressed.

03 — Process

Diagnosing a community
like a product funnel.

I ran a three-track diagnostic in parallel — analytics, strategy, and lived research — treating each as a data source that the others had to validate against. No single input drove decisions in isolation.

Track 01 — Quantitative

Full-funnel analytics audit

Analyzed Instagram analytics to identify where reach was high but conversion was low. Mapped the gap between content impressions, profile visits, link clicks, and event sign-ups — treating each as a funnel stage with its own drop-off problem to solve.

Track 02 — Strategic

Hyper-local content strategy

Translated the broader Acts2 Network design framework into tactics specific to the FJU campus vertical — bilingual content cadences, culturally relevant visual language, and an event calendar architected around the Taiwanese student academic rhythm.

Track 03 — Qualitative

1-on-1 mentorship as user research

Conducted regular 1-on-1 sessions with 10+ students — not as pastoral care, but as structured qualitative research. What made them come back? What almost made them leave? What did they wish existed? Their answers shaped every content and programming decision.

04 — AI-Native Execution

A lean team producing
agency-level output.

The constraint was real: a 10-person volunteer team with full-time jobs, expected to run a campus ministry, produce consistent digital content, plan monthly large-scale events, and grow a community — all simultaneously. AI wasn't a novelty; it was an operational necessity.

Force Multiplier
AI tools embedded across research, translation, content, and operations — not as a replacement for strategy, but as the infrastructure that made high-velocity execution possible with a lean team.
Research & Trends

Used Perplexity to rapidly synthesize campus engagement trends, benchmark against peer ministry communities, and identify content formats gaining traction with Taiwanese university students.

Bilingual Translation

Deployed AI as a Chinese-English translation layer — ensuring all event communications, social copy, and community messaging resonated accurately across both languages without requiring a dedicated translator.

Event Ideation

Used AI to rapidly generate and pressure-test event concepts against student interest data — cutting ideation cycles from days to hours and freeing the team to focus on execution and relationship-building.

Operations & Logistics

Templated recurring event planning workflows with AI assistance — venue research, logistics checklists, and communication sequences — so the team could run monthly large-scale events without reinventing the operational stack each time.

Global Perspective — Taipei → California

Designing for humans,
not assumptions.

Most designers talk about cross-cultural empathy. I lived it. Moving from California to Taipei — navigating a new language, a new cultural market, and a new community from scratch — is the sharpest possible test of whether your design instincts are genuinely universal or just familiar.

What I learned: the core human needs that drive community behavior — belonging, trust, recognition, forward momentum — are consistent across cultures. The surface expression changes; the underlying motivation doesn't. Designing for the surface gets you average work. Designing for the motivation gets you +185% reach.

That lens is now permanently part of how I approach product design. When I work on a California product team, I bring the fluency of someone who's had to question every assumption — including the ones that feel obvious.

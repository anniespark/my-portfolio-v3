02 — The Challenge

Design without a seat at the table.

National Pen's microsite powered high-stakes email and direct mail campaigns — and had been entirely neglected from a design standpoint. No dedicated designer. No dedicated dev. No stakeholder priority. Despite real traffic volume, it was converting poorly and nobody owned the problem.

The main site's checkout was a separate project with a dedicated team — one I wasn't on. My path in was the microsite: a constrained platform where I could operate independently, generate evidence, and use results to earn credibility for the larger opportunity.

"If the microsite results were strong enough, they'd open the door. So I made sure they were."

03 — Discovery

Research first. Then the fix.

Before proposing anything, I needed to understand the full picture. I ran a structured audit across three methods — heuristic evaluation, Hotjar behavioral data, and unmoderated user testing — synthesized through two workshops with the Experimentation Team and Stakeholders. The research pointed in one clear direction: mobile was the critical frontier.

Desktop — One structural flaw

Less friction than mobile overall, but one critical problem: the CTA was buried below the fold. Users who'd finished customization had to scroll past pre-filled information to find the action they came for. A solvable, scoped problem.

Mobile — Compounding friction

A multi-step flow never adapted for mobile. Hotjar showed high abandonment throughout. User testing revealed the specific moments users gave up — and why. Mobile traffic was also growing, making this the higher-impact problem to solve.

Three compounding heuristic violations emerged across the checkout arc — confirmed by both Hotjar behavioral data and direct user observation:

Visibility of System Status
No progress indicator across 8 checkout pages. Users couldn't see the end — fatigue-based abandonment was high. The fix had to be structural, not cosmetic.
Error Prevention
The "Place Order" CTA was positioned where mobile users accidentally triggered it mid-scroll. Rage-click data from Hotjar confirmed this pattern across device types.
Flexibility & Efficiency
Each page was dense with already-provided, pre-filled information. The CTA remained below the fold — users had to scroll past noise to find the action they came to take.
Mobile User Testing — Unmoderated · Real sessions
Abandonment triggers
  • → "Edit" on order review sends user back 5 steps — no warning
  • → Shipping + setup fees hidden until final review — price shock triggers abandonment
  • → Upsell modal has no confirmation — users frustrated enough to leave
Confusion points
  • → Tab titles read as nav bar — users click expecting navigation
  • → Static product preview — users try to zoom in, don't realize it's not interactive
  • → Imprint preview appears only after scroll — users don't know it exists
  • → Color change locked to step 2 — users want flexibility throughout

04 — Project 1: Microsite

Desktop first. Prove it. Then go mobile.

The research was clear: desktop had one solvable structural flaw, mobile had compounding systemic issues. I scoped the first test to the problem I could prove fastest — a sticky CTA bar on the desktop product landing page, deployable entirely through AB Tasty with zero engineering involvement. Tech and PM signed off in one meeting.

The hypothesis was tight — if the primary conversion action is always visible, late-funnel abandonment drops. Twelve days later, it was confirmed.

The desktop result did the political work. But more than unlocking the next test — it shifted how the team thought about design on the microsite entirely. I synthesized all research into a prioritized backlog: a set of A/B tests and critical fixes organized by impact and effort, with mobile at the top.

05 — Project 2: Main Site Checkout

Set the vision. Ship smart.

With a seat on the main checkout team, I started with the full vision: a one-page experience that compressed 8 checkout steps into a single continuous scroll. Not because I expected it to ship immediately — but because starting from the ideal is how you align a team on what "better" actually means. Post-launch measurement for the main site MVP is still in progress; the microsite numbers were the measurable proof point.

Before any concepts, the team mapped the checkout system as it actually existed. Four distinct payment portals — each with its own decision tree, UI, and abandonment risk. The complexity was structural. The system needed further qualitative and quantitative data to make confident simplification decisions.

"The one-page concept wasn't a failed attempt. It was the alignment tool."

The full re-architecture cleared design and PM review — then hit a real constraint: the engineering scope wasn't available in the current sprint. We were simultaneously running a Braintree payment infrastructure migration. That's not a blocker; it's context for smart scoping.

The MVP was designed around what was achievable within those real constraints — applying the same core principles as the ideal without restructuring the page architecture itself.

06 — Leadership

Proving design value without permission.

Strategic Approach

On the microsite, I acted as the sole design voice — partnering with PMs and SEM teams who'd grown accustomed to shipping without design input. Every proposal carried its own evidence, every test spoke in revenue language, and every result pointed toward the next opportunity.

A key insight from stakeholder workshops: returning customers and new prospects receive fundamentally different experiences via unique URL parameters, with prospect conversion in North America tracking lower. This shaped how tests were scoped — ensuring variant impact was measured against the right audience segment.

Research & Strategy — Oct 2024

Product Naming User Study

0/5
Participants compared product names when shopping. They used sort, filters, reviews, and photos instead.
Key Finding

Names like "Big Sur," "Bryce," and "Himalayan Stainless Steel" caused confusion or were ignored entirely. Users already scan for material descriptors.

Recommendation

Rename SKUs using material language — Soft-Touch, Stainless Steel, Recycled — that maps directly to how users already search and decide.

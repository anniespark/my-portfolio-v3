02 — The Challenge

Design without a seat at the table.

Pens.com has two ways to get customers through checkout:

  • —Main site — a full multi-step flow across their whole product catalog
  • —Microsite — a stripped-down single-product experience built for email and direct mail campaigns

Same needs across both:

  • —User need: an easy way to buy the product(s) they want
  • —Business need: reduce the abandonment that's costing the business

The main site checkout was the original project. But a Braintree infrastructure migration and a company-wide brand update pushed it out. While that resolved itself, I took initiative to shift focus to the microsite — for these reasons:

  • —It was pulling in solid revenue, yet there was no dedicated designer or dev team
  • —The lessons from this checkout could most likely be applied to the main site
  • —Eventually, it would make sense for both checkouts to share the same technology
  • —With a simpler platform and a real audience, we could experiment and get actual data points
03 — Discovery

Research first. Then the fix.

Before proposing anything, I needed to understand the full picture. I ran a structured audit across three methods — heuristic evaluation, Hotjar behavioral data, and unmoderated user testing — then brought the findings back to the Experimentation Team and stakeholders across two workshops. Everything pointed the same direction: mobile was clearly the bigger problem.

Less friction than mobile overall — one fixable problem: the CTA was buried below the fold. Users who'd finished customization had to scroll past pre-filled information to find the action they came for.

Mobile — Compounding friction

A multi-step flow that was never adapted for mobile. 10 screens to buy a single product — including two unexpected modal interruptions mid-flow. Hotjar showed high abandonment throughout, and user testing surfaced exactly where and why.

Five heuristic violations confirmed across the checkout — by Hotjar, unmoderated user testing, and direct observation:

Visibility of System Status
No progress indicator across 8 checkout pages. Users couldn't tell how far they were or how much was left — process fatigue set in fast. Abandonment wasn't random; it was a predictable response to being lost in a flow with no end in sight.
Error Prevention
On mobile, "Place Order" was positioned where users accidentally triggered it mid-scroll. Poor tap-target placement combined with no confirmation state on upsell actions meant users were making unintended purchases — or abandoning out of frustration when they realized it.
Flexibility & Efficiency of Use
The CTA was buried below the fold — especially on desktop. Worse, the flow forced users through every single step even when fields were already filled. No shortcuts, no quick wins. Users who'd done this before were penalized just as much as first-timers.
Aesthetic & Minimalist Design
Pages were long and cluttered with redundant information — high cognitive load at every step. One qualitative finding stood out: product names like "Big Sur" actively confused users. They ignored names entirely and relied on photos to judge quality instead.
Recognition Rather Than Recall
Users had to mentally carry selections from previous steps because the system never surfaced them. No persistent summary, no collapsed state. Users were recalling, not recognizing — and that cognitive cost compounded across 8 pages.

04 — Project 1: Microsite

Desktop first. Prove it. Then go mobile.

Desktop had one fixable problem — the CTA was buried. Mobile had several, layered on top of each other. I scoped the first test to what I could prove the fastest — a sticky CTA bar on the desktop product landing page, deployable entirely through AB Tasty with zero engineering involvement. Tech and PM signed off in one meeting.

The hypothesis was tight — if the primary conversion action is always visible, late-funnel abandonment drops. Twelve days later, it was confirmed.

+$15k
Revenue recovered in the first 12 days — zero engineering involvement, no prior stakeholder buy-in

The desktop result did the political work. But more than unlocking the next test — it shifted how the team thought about design on the microsite entirely. I synthesized all research into a prioritized backlog: a set of A/B tests and critical fixes organized by impact and effort, with mobile at the top.

Set the vision. Ship smart.

What I found wasn't one checkout — it was four distinct payment portals, each with its own decision tree, UI, and abandonment risk. It wasn't a UX problem sitting on top of a working system. The system itself had fractured into four separate things.

06 — Leadership

Proving design value without permission.

Strategic Approach

On the microsite, I was the only designer in the room — partnering with PMs and SEM teams who'd been shipping without design input for years. I made sure every proposal had evidence behind it and every result pointed to what was next. That's how I stayed in the room.

Key insight — audience segmentation

Returning customers and new prospects receive fundamentally different experiences via unique URL parameters — with prospect conversion in North America tracking lower. This shaped how every test was scoped: variant impact measured against the right audience segment, not aggregate traffic.

Throughout both projects, I was thinking about checkout as a system — not two isolated experiences. The microsite is a simplified single-product checkout; the main site is the full commerce flow. Different constraints, but the same user trying to get through the same process. That's the through-line that mattered.

Research & Strategy — Oct 2024

Product Naming User Study

0/5
Participants compared product names when shopping. They used sort, filters, reviews, and photos instead.
Key Finding

Names like "Big Sur," "Bryce," and "Himalayan Stainless Steel" caused confusion or were ignored entirely. Users already scan for material descriptors.

Recommendation

Rename SKUs using material language — Soft-Touch, Stainless Steel, Recycled — that maps directly to how users already search and decide.
